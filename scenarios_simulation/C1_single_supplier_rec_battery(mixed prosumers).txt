\subsection{Scenario C1: Single Supplier with REC and Battery Optimisation (Mixed Prosumers)}
\label{sec:results_C1}

This section presents the results of Scenario~C1, which extends the single-supplier REC configuration (Scenario~A2) with centralised battery storage optimisation. A two-stage Mixed-Integer Linear Programme (MILP), solved via Pyomo and Gurobi over a rolling 24-hour MPC horizon, determines optimal battery dispatch schedules subject to Pareto fairness constraints. Two battery assets are deployed: a 200~kWh community battery (BESS\_REC\_01, $\pm$68~kW) and an 80~kWh residential battery (BESS\_REC\_02, $\pm$25~kW), both with 95\% round-trip efficiency and SOC bounds of 20--100\%.

The internal REC transfer price follows feed-in parity ($p^{\mathrm{p2p}}_t = p^{\mathrm{exp}}_t$), ensuring prosumer indifference between internal and external sales while providing consumers with a price advantage over the retail import tariff.

\subsubsection{Battery Impact on REC Energy Costs}

The battery reduces external energy costs for REC members through a three-stage mechanism embedded in the simulation pipeline. First, battery optimisation runs \emph{after} DA/ID market scheduling but \emph{before} REC settlement. The MILP dispatches batteries to minimise the community's aggregate grid cost, and the resulting charge/discharge profiles are applied directly to the metered actuals: battery discharge is added to generation profiles and battery charging is added to load profiles.

Second, the REC settlement algorithm computes internally shared energy as $E^{\mathrm{shared}}_t = \min\!\big(\sum_i G_{i,t},\; \sum_j L_{j,t}\big)$, where generation $G$ now includes battery discharge. The inflated generation total increases the shared energy volume, meaning more consumer demand is covered internally rather than imported from the grid.

Third, supplier billing uses the REC-corrected meters. Each consumer's billable grid import is reduced by their proportional share of the internally shared energy. Since shared energy is settled at the reduced community grid access fee rather than the full retail tariff, each additional MWh of battery-enabled sharing translates directly into consumer cost savings.

In Scenario~C1, this mechanism redirects 34.08~MWh of energy from grid import (at retail price) to internal REC sharing, reducing the supplier's retail revenue by \EUR{178.04} relative to A2. The corresponding reduction in feed-in compensation ($-$\EUR{202.78}) confirms that the battery absorbs PV surplus that would otherwise be exported, converting an export-compensated flow into an internally shared flow. The net effect is a transfer of value from the supplier's margin to the REC members' energy bills.

\subsubsection{Balancing Performance}
\label{sec:C1_balancing}

Table~\ref{tab:C1_balancing} summarises the annual balancing group outcomes. The battery creates a deliberate structural imbalance of +1.57~MWh because the supplier's DA/ID schedules are based on load and generation forecasts that do not incorporate battery dispatch. This reflects realistic market sequencing: the supplier commits positions on the wholesale market \emph{before} the battery optimisation runs intraday. Since the MILP minimises community grid cost by shifting PV surplus into evening demand peaks, the actual metered flows diverge from the forecast-based schedule. Despite this, the imbalance penalty (\EUR{554.64}) is nearly fully offset by balancing rewards (\EUR{557.46}), yielding a negligible net balancing settlement of +\EUR{2.82}.

Compared with Scenario~A2 (single supplier with REC, mixed prosumers, no battery), where the imbalance is near-zero ($\approx$0~MWh), the +1.57~MWh structural imbalance in C1 is a direct consequence of the battery dispatch not being communicated back to the supplier's wholesale schedule. This is a deliberate modelling choice: in practice, REC-level battery optimisation operates on shorter time horizons (intraday) than the supplier's DA/ID commitments, creating an information asymmetry between the scheduled and actual balancing group positions. The dual-pricing balancing mechanism ensures that the financial impact remains symmetrically distributed---the supplier is rewarded when the imbalance helps the system and penalised when it does not, resulting in a near-zero net settlement.

The net profit increase of \EUR{27.21} (+0.11\%) relative to A2 is modest and primarily reflects two offsetting effects:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Revenue uplift (+\EUR{307.35}):} Higher balancing rewards (+\EUR{485.38}) partially offset slightly lower retail sales ($-$\EUR{178.04}). Wholesale revenue is unchanged.

    \item \textbf{Cost increase (+\EUR{280.14}):} Higher balancing penalties (+\EUR{482.91}) partially offset lower feed-in compensation ($-$\EUR{202.78}). Market purchases are unchanged.
\end{enumerate}

The small net improvement suggests that, under the current market design and pricing parameters, the battery's primary contribution is to increase community self-sufficiency (shared energy +35.9\%) and reduce REC members' external energy costs rather than to improve supplier profitability directly. The Pareto fairness constraint prevents the battery from pursuing aggressive wholesale arbitrage strategies that might increase supplier profit at the expense of individual participants. The negligible net balancing settlement (+\EUR{2.82}) confirms that the deliberate structural imbalance introduced by battery dispatch carries no meaningful financial risk for the supplier.
